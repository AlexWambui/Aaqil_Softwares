Feyman's study technique

Feyman was a physicist "quantumelectrodynamics" and was a great explainer that infact that became his nickname.

Teach what you're learning to somebody else becuase explaining helps you better understand a concept.

A step by step process for doing this is called the Feyman's technique.

Feyman's first principle is that you must not fool yourself and you're the easiest person to fool.

The process has four steps:

  1. Take a piece of paper and write the concept's name at the top.
  2. Explain the concept using simple language as if teaching it to someone else. Also work through examples and ensure you practice as well.
  3. Identify problem areas (those you're shaky on) then go back to the sources to review untill your understanding becomes solid.
  4. Look at your explanation and try to Identify any complicated terms (or convoluted language) and challenge yourself to simplify them into something easy to understand. Like you were explaining it to a kid by asking why all the time. Like why does this or that work. Like instead of just knowing a formula, know why it works.

This concept helps you understand where your knowledge is solid and pinpoints where it's shaky and needs to put in some extra work.

9 Types of intelligence

The original types were seven but two more were added by Howard Gardener over the years.

  1. Visual-Spatial intelligence - Has to do with how well someone is at manoeuvring through space and visualizing things. These people are good at identifying charts and graphs and they are good map readers. They have a good sense of direction. Careers include: architecture, computer science and design. To cultivate this intelligence, take photography or drawing classes, draw mind maps of your plans or ideas, do puzzles and mazes for fun. Famous people with this intelligence include Steve Jobs.
  2. Linguistic verbal intelligence - Is all about words. This translates to great readers, writers and speakers. Generally these people can tell a good story and memorize words quickly and express their feelings easily to others. To cultivate this intelligence, write a journal, play word games like scrable and cross word puzzles, read more books and debate issues more often. Careers include: writers, public speakers, lawyers.
  3. Logical-Mathematical intelligence - Is about logical problem solving and number sense. People with this intelligence are obviously good at solving math problems and are strong conceptual thinkers. Think of scientists and mathematicians. Careers involve: Scientists, Computer Programmers, inventors. To cultivate this intelligence play log and pattern games like sudoku, find the pattern in a poem, develop a hypothesis, organize a collection. Famous people with this intelligence include Nicola Tesla and Bill Gates.
  4. Bodily-Kinesthetic intelligence - Is how well people can move through space. People with this intelligence have excellent control of how they move their bodies in space and high body awareness. Careers include: dancers, construction workers, actors, sculptors. These people have good eye-hand cordination. To cultivate this intelligence, indulge in physical activities, manipulate objects, build things. Famous people with this intelligence include: Michael Jordan and Michael jackson.
  5. Musical intelligence - Can you clap to the beat and sing in tune? People with above average musical intelligence can recognize tones and hear patterns in songs. They are drawn to both listening and creating songs. Careers include: DJ, band director, singer. To cultivate this intelligence, listen to music while studying, connect music to a lesson, write a short song. Famous people with this intelligence include Mozart and Alicia Keys.
  6. Interpersonal intelligence - Is about interpersonal or social skills. It's about being empathetic and good at understanding other peoples intentions and emotions. These people excel at group work and keeping the peace in organizations, they are excellent communicators and sensitive to other people's needs, they can also see other people's perspectives. Can you figure out what other people's body language means? Do you tend to know what people are thinking or feeling without having to ask? Careers include: politicians, teachers, social workers, counselors. To cultivate this intelligence, engage in group work, volunteer in community service, teach a class or a friend. Famous people with this intelligence include: Oprah, Dr. Phil and Mother Teressa.
  7. Intrapersonal intelligence - This is self awareness. It's about how good you are at reflecting on and being aware of your own mental and emotional state at any given time. These are the philosophers and the day dreamers. To cultivate this intelligence, keep a daily journal, read self-help books, imagine yourself in other people's shoes.
  8. Naturalistic intelligence - These are nature lovers. They are senstive to slight changes in their environment and gravitate to exploring nature. Careers involve, zoo keepers, conservationists. To cultivate this intelligence, keep a nature journal, start a small garden, go outside for everyday activities, keep a pet.
  9. Pedagogical intelligence - These are the effortless teachers. People who can instruct, facilitate and convey information to others. It's one thing to understand a topic, but it's a very different skill set to help other people understand that same topic. Famous people with this intelligence include: Budha.

Thinking you're strong in one intelligence may limit the effort you put into learning the others.

Looking at skills and abilities as changeable through hard work and practice enables us to change those abilities. This is the growth mindset.

When we think that our abilities are innate, it is less likely that we can improve.

People have different ways of learning: visual, aural, read/write, kinesthetic.

Socializing.

  • Understand you're not born socially handicapped - we are social beings. The reason you may not be so good at interactions, is that you haven't given your brain enough time to observe interactions and become fluent in all the patterns and rules. The human brain is actually very good at identifying patterns and rules when it comes to social interactions thus we can purely learn from observation. Things you have to pay attention to when having a conversation with someone include: body language, facial expressions, tonality, speed of talking, proximity, eye contact, etc. So just practice by interacting with more people no matter how the shallow the interaction might be. You can try the 100 people challenge where you interact with a 100 people for a span of 1 month. You can just say hi and that would also count as an interaction. Watching videos can help but the best way to learn is to do the talking in practice.
