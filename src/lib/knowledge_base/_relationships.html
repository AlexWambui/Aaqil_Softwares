The Five Love Languages

#5 Physical Touch

Research on child development has concluded that babies who are held, hugged, and touched tenderly develop a healthier emotional life than those who are left for long periods of time without physical contanct. The same is true for the elderly.

In nursing homes, those who receive touch have a more positive spirit and generally do better than those who are not touched.

"We don't let people know the fact that we all like to be touched because we're afraid that people will misinterpret. So we sit back in loneliness and physical isolation."

The body is made for touching.

Tiny tactile receptors are located throughout the body. When those receptors are touched or pressed, nerves carry impulses to the brain, the brain interprets these impulses and we perceive that the thing that touched is warm, cold, hard or soft. It causes pain or pleasure. We may also interpret it as either loving or hostile touch.

The tips of our fingers, tip of the tongue and tip of our nose are higly sensitive.

Physical touch can make or break a relationship. It can communicate love or hate.

For someone whose love language is physical touch, your touch will speak much more than the words "I love you" or "I hate you". Withold touches and you isolate and raise doubts about your love.

When your body is touched, you are touched much more deeply than the mere physical contact. When someone withdraws from your body, they distance themselves from you emotionally.

Learn from the person you are touching what they perceive as a loving touch.

Physical touch is a poweful communicator of love. In a time of crisis, more than anything we need to feel loved. We can't always change events, but we can survive if we feel loved.

Crises provide a unique opportunity for expressing love. Your tender touches are remembered long after the crisis has passed. Your failure to touch may also never be forgotten.

A pat on the back, kiss on the cheek, a tender touch to the arm, holding hands and embracing are all dialects of the love language, physical touch.

For some, physical touch is not a primary love language so they tend to stiffen or withdraw when touched.

The purpose of love is to enhance the well-being or another, not to satisfy your own desires. Therefore, learning to speak another person's primary love language is the most effective way of loving others.

Today's single adult lives in the cultural aftermath of the sexual revolution that began half a century ago. The revolution found its roots in the early 1900s' writings of Sigmund Freud, the father of psychoanalysis.

Freud emphasized the effect of sex on behavior. According to him, the full and uninhibited satisfaction of all instinctual desires would create mental health and happiness.

Once decade before the 1960s' sexual revolution explode, Erich Fromm, a student of Freud chose to oppose his teacher in his work The Art of Loving, he wrote, "The obvious clinical facts demonstrate that men and women who devote their lives to unrestricted sexual satisfaction do not attain happiness, and very often suffer from severe neurotic conflicts or symptoms. The complete satisfaction of all instictual need is not only not a basis for happiness, it does not even guarantee sanity."

Piritim Sorokin, a leading sociologist of his day, predicted if Freud's ideas were applied to society, it would destroy the meaning of human sexuality.

Glenn Stanton, a social research analyst says, "With the great divorce between marriage and sexuality, the dream of a fulfilling sex life is more elusive to more people than it has been at any time in our history." His conclussion: Sex does not need to be liberated, rather it simply needs to be confined to its proper and most productive domain.

Decades of research show that this place is lifelong, monogamous marriage.

The commonly held idea that cohabitation will lead to a healthier marriage has been shattered by numerous research projects. The studies have found that those who cohabit before marriage have substantially higher divorce rates than those who do not. Prof. Jan Stets of Washington State University concluded, "Cohabiting couples compared to married couples have less healthy relationships. They have lower relationship quality, lower stability, and a higher level of disagreements."

The popular idea is that sex is a biological need on the same level as thirst. If thirsty, drink water. If hungry, eat food. If you have sexual desire, fulfill it.

Professor Soronkin's prophecy has come true: Sex has been reduced to mere copulation.

Sex is intimate and should be shared with someone to whom we have a deep commitment. When our sexual relationship is not exclusive, we feel violated.

The cohaviting male is four times more likely to be unfaithful as the married man, and the cohabiting woman is eight times more likely to be unfaithful to her "lover" as a married woman.

There is a reason why Christianity and most major world religions have a high regard for human sexuality, viewing it not as a biological drive on the level with thirst, but as a gift from God to be fully and freely expressed between a man and woman who are committed to each other by the covenant of marriage. All the sociological, anthropological, and psychological research of the past fifty years has validated this view of human sexuality.

The single adult in contemporary society must make the choice between Freud and the facts: uninhibited sexual expression or reserving sexual intercourse for the one to whom you are willing to make a lifelong commitment. This is no minor choice. It affects your physical and emotional health, as well as sexual satisfaction, for years to come.

Discover you primary love language.

One of the things that sets man apart from animals is his ability to communicate by means of words. Language is distinctively human.

Each of us has a primary love language and it's the one that speaks most deeply to us emotionally.

1. Ask yourself: How you typically express love and appreciation to other people.

At times what you do for other mostly like words of affirmation may be your primary love language because you wish for others to do the same for you.

About 25 % of adults speak one love language but wish to receive another language.

For the other 75%, the language they speak most often is the language they desire. They love in the manner they would like to be loved.

2, Observe what you request of others.

If you frequently ask for help with projects, acts of service maybe your love language.

Our requests tend to indicate our emotional needs. Therefore, observing what you request of others may clearly reveal your primary love language.

3. Listen to your complaints.

Our complaints reveal our deep emotional hurts.

The opposite of what hurts you is probably your love language.

If you received love in that language, the hurt would go away and you would feel appreciated.

4. Ask the right questions

What do you like most about the person you're dating?

What do they say that makes you desire to be with them?

Your answer will be very enlightening.

Other questions include:

What would be an ideal spouse for you?

If you could have the perfect mate, what would they be like?

Your picture of a perfect mate should give you some idea of your primary love language.

If not in a relationship, ask:

What do I want most in a friendship?

An ideal friend would ____.

Your answer will probably reveal your primary love language.

Discovering your own love language helps you understand why you feel more loved and appreciated by certain people than you do others.

How to discover other love language?

1. Observe their expressions, complaints and requests.

Observe how they express their love to other people.

Ask yourself, "What do they complain about most often?"

Notice what they request most often.

2. Margo's Discovery: Don't be afraid to ask questions.

If you wish to know what is going on inside the mind of someone else, then ask questions.

The questions must be well chosen and must be expressions of genuine desire for information.

Take the story of Margo asking her mom what she'd like most on her birthday. The mother replies with nothing being any better than spending a whole day with Margo and this reveals her love language is quality time.

3. Helen's Discovery: Good gifts are different for everyone.

You can use a statement such as: You've really done so much and I'd like to do something to show you how much I appreciate what you've done. What can I do?

4. Experiment a little bit.

Another to discover someone's love language is to try out different things to see what works.

It takes time, effort and though to discover another person's primary love language.

Love languages and your special someone.

Many singles have given up on dating. They find it to be road strewn with heartache, physical frustration, misunderstanding, and untold anxiety, all of which add up to a "why bother?" attitude. Yet for others, the very idea of not dating sounds unnatural.

Dating today has many pitfalls but that doesn't mean the process itself is necessarily evil. On the contrary, it may be one of the most healthy and beneficial social systems in our entire society.

The reason many singles have failed in the dating game is that they have never clearly understood their objectives.

Many think of the purpose of dating as "to have a good time" to "to find a mate". In some general sense, we know that the end of all this may lead us to marriage, but we are not clear as to other specific objectives.

1. Develop wholesome interactions with the opposite sex.

One of the purposes of dating is to get to know member of the opposite sex and to learn to relate to them as individual people.

Half of the world is made up of the opposite sex. If you fail to learn the art of building wholesome relationships with "the other half", you have immediately and considerably limited your horizons.

Our differences are numerous, but our basic needs are the same.

Serving people is life's highest calling. To serve people, we must know them - male and female.

Relationships cannot be built without some kind of special interaction, today, dating provides the setting for such interaction.

One of our chief stumbling blocks to healthy dating relationships is that we have trained to view each other as sex objects.

Erick Fromm "What most people in our culture mean by being lovable is essentially a mixture between being popular and having sex appeal".

With a proliferation of TV programmes and the internet as a lifestyle, the perception of others as sex objects has become deeply ingrained in our thinking.

giving your attention to the wide array of pornographic materials that are now readily available anywhere there is an Internet connection make you often find yourself addicted to this impersonal, disconnected perception of members of the opposite sex. When this becomes a fixed perception, then one ceases in the truest sense to be human. He or she becomes like an animal playing with his toys or allowing one’s self to be a toy with which another animal plays.

2. Learn about the person, personality and philosophy

Dating provides an opportunity to break down the perceptions of each other that the world has built up, and to learn to see others as persons rather than objects.

It is in dating that we can learn names, personalities, and philosophies. These are the qualities of personhood.

The name - identifies us as a unique person. Personality reveals the nature of our uniquness. Philosophy reveals the values by which we live our lives. All these become more clear as we begin to interact more personally with each other.

It is in dating that we discover evey woman has a mother and father, and so does every man. Know or unknown, living or dead, our parents have influenced us and thus profoundly affected who we are. We are all connected with our past. In the dating relationship, we have the potential for excavating these roots.

Our society pushes us to live in cocoons of social media and lonely commutes. This isolation has brough us to growing levels of loneliness, emptiness and sometimes desperation. Dating is an acceptable way of breaking out of isolation and connecting with others.

3. See our own strengths and weaknesses.

Dating also helps in aiding one's own personality.

All of us are in process. We ought to wear signs around our necks reading: "under construction."

As we relate to others in the dating context, we begin to exhibit various personality traits. This provokes healthy self-analysis and brings greater self-understanding. We recognize that some traits are more desirable than others. We see our own strengths and weaknesses.

The acknowledgement of a weakness is the first step toward growth.

No one is perfect. Maturity is not flawlessness. However, we are never to be satisfied with our present status of development.

What we dislike in others is often a weakness in our own lives.

In getting to know others, you can write the things you intend to say or ask about their lives beforehand.

Change takes effort, but it is effort well invested.

4. Practice serving others.

History is replete with examples of men and women who discovered that humanity's greatest contribution is in giving to others.

True greatness is expressed in serving.

This service however is Ministry serving not martyrdom (Poor ol' me. I have to do this service as a duty).

Ministry is something we do for others, whereas martyrdom is something others bring upon us.

Dating is always a two-way street. Certainly we receive something from the relationship, but we are also to be contributing to the life of the person we are dating.

In dating, many a reserved fellow could be "drawn out" by the wise questions of a dating partner. Many a hothead could be calmed by the truth spoken in love.

Genuine service demands that we speak the truth in love. We do not serve each other by avoiding one another's weaknessses.

Empathetic listening is an awesome medication for the hurting heart.

At times we are in lives of others just to serve them for a season, like when someone is grieving their lost parent.

5. Discover the person we will marry.

Dating is designed to help us gain a realistic idea of the kind of person we need as a marriage partner.

Dating people with differing personalities gives us criteria for making wise judgements.

Someone who has limited dating experience may, after marriage, be plagued with thoughts like, "What are other women / men like?" or "Would I have had a better marriage with another type of mate?" But the individual who looks back on a well-rounded social life before marriage is better equiped to answer these questions. He is not as likely to build a dream world, becuase experience has taught him that all of us are imperfect.

Dating can be taken as a way of finding someone with whom we can live in harmony and fulfilment for the next fifty years. The variables are great.

It's true that opposites attract, but opposites may also repel. This is why couples can be so attracted before marriage and so disillusioned afterwards.

The reality is, the more similar we are, the fewer conflicts we will have.

Similarity is especially important when it comes to the bigger issues of life: value, spirituality, morals, whether or not to have children, how many children to have and vocational goals.

Dating provides the context for exploring answers to these questions and determining our suitability for marriage.

An attitude of love should motivate you to want to relate to others as persons rather than objects, to develop your own personality so you can reach your potential for good in the world, and to serve your dating partner and seek to encourage that person to reach his / her potential.

Learning to express love in a language your dating partner will understand is critical. When the dating partner feels loved, he or she is much more likely to be open to an authentic relationship.

A marital relationship designed to last a lifetime requires more than these euphoric, obsessive feelings.

6. A time to talk about the real stuff

We must not allow the euphoria to bling us from seeing the glaring difference between us on the fundamental issues.

If too far apart on the fundamental issues (value, morals, spirituality, social interests, vocational visions and the desire or lack of desire to have children), be wise enough to express appreciation for the contribution made to the other's life and then go separate ways.

To marry in the height of the "in love" euphoria and ignore these more fundamental issues is to set one's self up for a painful and difficult marriage.

Good marriages are built on a combination of emotional love and a common commitment to a core of beliefs about what is most important and what we wish to do with our lives.

Speaking each other's primary love language creates the emotional climate where these beliefs can be fleshed out in daily life.

6. Economic advantage

7. Security

While these objectives can be accomplished outside of marriage, abundant research has indicated that married people are happier, healthier, and better off financially.

The purpose of marriage though, runs deeper than these seven goals.

A deeper purpose

In the ancient biblical account of Creation, God says of Adam, “It is not good for the man to be alone.” God’s answer to man’s need was “I will make a helper suitable for him.” The Hebrew word for suitable literally means “faceto-face.” The picture is that God created one with whom man could have a face-to-face relationship.

It speaks of that kind of in-depth, personal relationship whereby the two are united in an unbreakable union that satisfies the deepest longings of the human heart.

Marriage is God's answer for humanity's deepest need - union of life with another.

Indeed, that same ancient account of creation says of Adam and Eve, "They will become one flesh".

Man's psychological history is replete with his desire for connection. Marriage is designed to be the most intimate of all human relationships.

The couple shares life intellectually, emotionally, socially, physically and spiritually and they share life to such a degree that they become "one flesh".

This does not mean couples lose their individuality, it means they have a deep sense of unity.

This kind of union does not come without a deep enduring commitment.

Marriage is not a contract to make sexual relationships legal. It is not merely a social institution to provide for the care of children. It is not merely a psychological clinic where we gain the emotional support we need. It is not a means of gaining social status or economic security. The ultimate purpose of marriage is not even achieved when it is the vehicle for love and companionship, as valuable as these are.

The supreme purpose of marriage is the union of a man and woman at the deepest possible level and in all areas of life, which in turn brings the greatest possible sense of fulfillment to the couple and best serves the purposes of God for their lives.

The act of getting married does not just give a couple this kind of unity. There is a difference between “being united” and “unity".

If our goal is oneness, then the key question before marriage ought to be “What reasons do we have for believing that we can become one?” As we examine the intellectual, social, emotional, spiritual, and physical areas of life, what do we find? Do we hold enough in common in these areas to provide a foundation for unity?

No house should be built without a suitable foundation. Likewise, no marriage should be initiated until the couple has explored their foundation.

This means couples ought to spend time discussing each basic area of life in order to determine who they are.

Intellectual Unity

To explore intellectual unity, you need to get very practical.

Set aside specific dating time to discuss with each other the kind of books you read. This reveals something of your intellectual interest.

If one doesn't read books, this is also revealing. reading newspapers regularly, reading magazines, TV shows they enjoy most, web sites they frequent.

The answers to these questions indicate something of your intellectual interest.

Grades in school and the amount of education each has should also be considered.

It's not a must that you have the same areas of intellectual interests and it's not about perfection, but you ought to be able to communicate with each other on the same intellectual plane.

Do you hold enough in common intellectually to have a basis for growth? This can be answered by attempting some growth exercises.

Agree to read some books and spend some quality time discussing its concepts. Once in a while, read the headline article at your news website of choice and discuss its merits and implications. This reveals a great deal regarding your personal status and potential for future growth in intellectual intimacy.

Social Unity

We are all social creatures, but our social interests will often differ greately.

Games and sports interest, musical interests, recreational activities, parties and if so, what kind of parties. These are questions you can't afford not to answer.

It's not a must to have the same social interests but you must have a foundation for unity. Hold enough in common that you can begin to grow together.

Stretch yourself, go to things you haven't learnt to enjoy before, see if you can learn to enjoy some of the same things.

If interests differ, ask yourself, if he never changes his present social interest, will I be happy to live with him the rest of my life?

Can you write a descriptive paragraph about the kind of person you are? (about your personality). Discuss your self-concept as compared with how you appear to others.

What do you as a potential problem area when you think of living life together? Can you make progress in overcoming these difficulties before marriage?

Unresolved problems before marriage are often magnified after marriage.

Persnalities don't have to be identical - this could make a pretty boring marriage. There should however, be a basic understanding of each other's personality and some idea of how you will relate to each other.

Emotional Unity

The euphoria of the "in love" experience leads couples to feeling like they have genuine emotional intimacy. However, when the euphoria subsides, some couples discover that the foundation for emotional intimacy is extremely weak. They experience feelings of estrangement and distance.

Emotional intimacy is that deep sense of being connected to one another. It is feeling loved, respected and appreciated, while at the same time seeking to reciprocate.

The three ingredients of emotional unity: feeling loved, respect and appreciation.

To feel loved is to have the sense that the other person genuinely cares about your well-being.

Respect has to do with feeling that your potential spouse has positive regard for your personhood, intellect, abilites and personality. Respect begins with the attitude "I acknowledge that you are a creature of extreme worth. God has endowed you with certain abilities and emotions. Therefore I respect you as a person. I will not desecrate your worth by making critical remarks about your intellect, your judgement, or your logic. I will seek to understand you and grant you the freedom to think differently from the way I think and to experience emtions that I may not experience." Respect means that you give the other person the freedom to be an individual. You can tell if someone respects you by the way they treat your ideas, emotions and dreams.

Appreciation is the innter sense that your partner values your contribution to the relationship. Each of us expends our energy and abilities in ways that benefit our relationship. To sense that our potential mate recognizes our efforts and appreciates them builds emotional intimacy between the two of us. Appreciation can look like, complimenting each other by saying things like "Thanks for calling me when you realized you were going to be late. It means a lot to me that you were thinking about me" or "Thanks for inviting me over for a meal. I know how much time and energy it takes to prepare a meal like this. I want you to know that I really appreciate your hard work, and the meal was delicious." If your thoughful acts go unnoticed, you may begin to feel unappreciated and emotional distance develops. Appreciation can also focus on abilities. "I love to hear you sing. You are so talented." or personality: "I am so grateful for your positive spirit about things. I know you were disappointed last night when I had to cancel our date, but it made me feel so much better when you told me you understood." Appreciation requires concentration. You must observe the other person's actions, words, attitudes and personality. Then take initiative to express your gratitude.

If there's genuine love, respect and appreciation, then you will experience emotional unity.

Share what makes you feel loved, resepected and appreciated.

Spiritual Unity

Spiritual foundations are often the least excavated, even by couple who attend church regularly.

Most couples find their greatest disappointment in marriage is that there is so little unity in this area. "we never pray together" or "church is like something we do individually. Even though we sit together, we never discuss what we experience".

Too many premarital disucussions on religion deal only with church attendance and other external matters. They fail to grapple with the most basic and important issues.

It's not just about church membership, charitable giving, or family tradition. It's about the spiritual foundation for marriage. Do you each agree there is an infinite, personal God? Do you know this God? These questions get to the heart of the matter.

Are you encouraging each other in spiritual growth or is one gently but consistently pullin in the opposite direction?

Spiritual foundations are the most important becuase they influence all other areas of life and unity.

Physical Unity

Sexual unity can't be separated from emotional, spiritual and social unity. In fact, problems that develop in the sexual aspect of marriage almost always have their root in one of these other areas.

Entering marriage without a physical examination is like playing Russian roulette. Then you have realistically face the implications of such a disease.

Research indicates, "monogamous individuals committed to one lifetime partner are the most physicaly and emotionally satisfied people sexually."

Most people who engage in sexual relationships outside of marriage do so out of a sincere desire to find intimacy. Unfortunately, sexual intercouse does not create intimacy and becomes itself a source of great pain physically and emotionally.

Don't allow past failures to cause you to give up. Losing a batlle doesn't mean the war is lost. We can't retrace our steps and we can't undo the past. We can, however, chart our couse for the future. Don't excuse present behaviour for past failure. Confess your wrong and accept God's forgiveness.

Dealing with scars

God forgives, but the natural results of our behaviors are not totally removed.

Like a man slamming their car into a telephone pole due to anger, resulting in a broken arm. They have God's forgiveness before going to the hospital but the arm is still broken and the car is lost.

In our moral behaviour, scars of failure are not totally removed by confession.

Bibilical challenge is honesty in all things. Disclose fully what happened in your past.

Marriage has no closets for skeletons. Your past is your past and can never be changed. Trust your partner to accept you as you are, not as he or she might with you were. If such acceptance cannot be experienced, then marriage should not be consummated. You must enter marriage with all the cards on the table.

In addition to acceptance of your potential mate, accept yourself and overcome your own past. This may involve counselling and certainly exploration of spiritual healing. For the christian, understand what the scriptures say about our sexuality. One cannot come away from such a study without the impression that the bibilical view of sexual intercourse withing marriage is positive. It is wholesome, beautiful and ordained of God. An understading of truth will liberate you from negative attitudes.

You are not destined to fail in marriage because of past failures. You will have roadblocks to overcome that would not be there if your had followed God's ideal.

Relationship Questions.

Question to increase closeness

Section I

  1. Given the choice of anyone in the world, whom would you want as a dinner guest?
  2. Would you like to be famous? In what way?
  3. Before making a telephone call, do you ever rehearse what you are going to say? Why?
  4. What would constitute a “perfect” day for you?
  5. When did you last sing to yourself? To someone else?
  6. If you were able to live to the age of 90 and retain either the mind or body of a 30-year-old for the last 60 years of your life, which would you want?
  7. Do you have a secret hunch about how you will die?
  8. Name three things you and your partner appear to have in common.
  9. For what in your life do you feel most grateful?
  10. If you could change anything about the way you were raised, what would it be?
  11. Take four minutes and tell your partner your life story in as much detail as possible.
  12. If you could wake up tomorrow having gained any one quality or ability, what would it be?

Section II

  1. If a crystal ball could tell you the truth about yourself, your life, the future, or anything else, what would you want to know?
  2. Is there something that you’ve dreamed of doing for a long time? Why haven’t you done it?
  3. What is the greatest accomplishment of your life?
  4. What do you value most in a friendship?
  5. What is your most treasured memory?
  6. What is your most terrible memory?
  7. If you knew that in one year you would die suddenly, would you change anything about the way you are now living? Why?
  8. What does friendship mean to you?
  9. What roles do love and affection play in your life?
  10. Alternate sharing something you consider a positive characteristic of your partner. Share a total of five items.
  11. How close and warm is your family? Do you feel your childhood was happier than most other people’s?
  12. How do you feel about your relationship with your mother?

Section III

  1. Make three true “we” statements each. For instance, “We are both in this room feeling…”
  2. Complete this sentence: “I wish I had someone with whom I could share…”
  3. If you were going to become a close friend with your partner, please share what would be important for them to know.
  4. Tell your partner what you like about them; be very honest this time, saying things that you might not say to someone you’ve just met.
  5. Share with your partner an embarrassing moment in your life.
  6. When did you last cry in front of another person? By yourself?
  7. Tell your partner something that you like about them [already].
  8. What, if anything, is too serious to be joked about?
  9. If you were to die this evening with no opportunity to communicate with anyone, what would you most regret not having told someone? Why haven’t you told them yet?
  10. Your house, containing everything you own, catches fire. After saving your loved ones and pets, you have time to safely make a final dash to save any one item. What would it be? Why?
  11. Of all the people in your family, whose death would you find most disturbing? Why?
  12. Share a personal problem and ask your partner’s advice on how they might handle it. Also, ask your partner to reflect back to you how you seem to be feeling about the problem you have chosen.
